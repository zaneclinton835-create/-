# Reid Hoffman Key Decisions

## 1. LinkedIn Founding (2002)

### Context
- After PayPal (co-founded 1998, sold to eBay 2002)
- Saw opportunity: professional identity online
- Allen Tovey, Konstantin Guerot joined

### Network Effects Logic

**Decision**: Build LinkedIn as professional network

**Reasoning**:
> "Professional identity is unique. Unlike social life, you can't have two professional identities. This makes switching costs enormous."

**Key Decisions**:
1. **Real names required** - Identity moat
2. **Connections public** - Network visibility
3. **Invitation only initially** - Quality control
4. **Slow early growth** - Network effects take time

**Outcome**:
- LinkedIn reached 1M users by 2004
- IPO 2011, $9B valuation at launch
- Now Microsoft acquisition $26B

### Decision Framework
1. Can this create network effects?
2. What's the chicken-and-egg problem?
3. How do we solve liquidity?

## 2. PayPal Decision: eBay Acquisition (2002)

### Context
- PayPal had 5M users, growing 15% weekly
- eBay wanted to integrate payments
- Hoffman had to decide: sell or stay independent

### Network Effects Analysis

**Decision**: Sell to eBay

**Reasoning**:
> "PayPal's network effects were strongest on eBay. 25% of eBay auctions paid with PayPal. The network was already integrated."

**Logic**:
- Vertical integration with eBay strengthens payments network
- Better to own payment flow than fight platform
- Focus on what networks you're strongest in

**Counterfactual**: Could have IPO'd but with more risk

### Decision Framework
- Which network is dominant?
- Double-click or go deeper?
- Strengthen existing network vs. expand to new

## 3. Joining Greylock Partners (2009)

### Context
- After Microsoft/LinkedIn transition
- Wanted to invest in network-effect businesses
- Greylock: partner model, focus on early-stage

### Network Effects Logic

**Decision**: Join as partner

**Reasoning**:
> "As an investor, I can apply network effects thinking to many companies, not just LinkedIn."

**Investment Thesis**:
- Find companies building network effects
- Apply blitzscaling at right moment
- Use network to help portfolio

**Key Investments**:
- Airbnb
- Instagram
- Dropbox
- And others

### Decision Framework
1. Can I leverage my network?
2. Does this create network effects for others?
3. Is this the right timing?

## 4. Microsoft Acquisition (2016)

### Context
- LinkedIn stagnant at $15-20B valuation
- Satya Nadella reached out
- Professional network + enterprise software synergy

### Network Effects Logic

**Decision**: Accept $26.2B acquisition

**Reasoning**:
> "Microsoft + LinkedIn = professional network + enterprise tools. The network effects become stronger."

**Synergies**:
- LinkedIn identity → Office, Outlook, Windows
- Enterprise data → LinkedIn recruiting
- Network grows across more surfaces

**Criticism**: Sold out independence

**Hoffman's Defense**:
> "Network effects are stronger now with Microsoft's distribution."

## 5. Career: Apple/Fujitsu to PayPal

### Context
- MBA from Stanford
- Product manager at Apple
- Executive at Fujitsu
- Left to co-found SocialNet (first startup)

### Decision Logic

**Pattern**: Always toward networks

**SocialNet (1997)**:
- Online dating/social network
- Too early, didn't work
- Learned network effects lesson

**PayPal (1998)**:
- Email-based payments
- Network effects: senders + receivers
- Email as network, money as value

**Key Insight**:
> "SocialNet failed because I didn't understand network effects deeply enough. PayPal succeeded because email already had network effects."

## Common Decision Patterns

### The Network Test
For any decision, Hoffman asks:
1. Does this strengthen network effects?
2. Does this add more nodes or more edges?
3. Does this increase switching costs?

### The Flywheel Check
- Will this ignite the flywheel?
- What triggers acceleration?
- When does flywheel become self-sustaining?

### The Liquidity Question
- Does this increase market liquidity?
- Can supply meet demand efficiently?
- Is there a chicken-and-egg problem?

### The Blitzscaling Decision
When to blitzscale:
1. You have product-market fit
2. You have a lead
3. The market is winner-take-all
4. You can raise capital to fund it

When NOT to blitzscale:
1. Product isn't ready
2. Market is fragmented
3. You don't have a lead
4. No capital available

## Key Quotes on Decisions

> "Make decisions with 70% of the information. Waiting is also a decision."

> "The question isn't whether to expand. It's whether you can accelerate the flywheel."

> "In network businesses, you're either growing or dying. There's no equilibrium."

> "Trust the network. If your network thinks it's a good idea, probably is."

> "The best decisions align with network effects."
